Petrov-Galerkin Finite Element Method: What Is Weak Formulation?

The Finite Element Method (FEM) is a powerful computational technique used to approximate the solutions of boundary value problems in engineering and physical sciences. Its development is closely linked to the concept of weak formulation, an essential mathematical approach that broadens the class of functions that can be considered solutions.

Traditionally, differential equations underlying physical phenomena are expressed in what is known as the "strong form." This form requires solutions to satisfy the differential equation and boundary conditions exactly at every point within the domain of interest. However, finding such solutions analytically is often impractical or impossible for complex problems.

The weak formulation is a pivotal concept that comes into play by relaxing these stringent requirements. Instead of demanding that the solution satisfy the differential equation exactly everywhere, the weak formulation requires the solution to satisfy the equation in an integral or "average" sense over the domain. This approach not only simplifies the problem but also allows for the inclusion of functions that are not differentiable in the traditional sense, thereby expanding the scope of potential solutions.

The transition from strong to weak formulations is achieved through a process known as "testing" the differential equation against a set of functions known as test functions. By integrating the product of the differential equation and these test functions over the domain, one ensures that the equation holds in an averaged sense. The choice of test functions is crucial, and this is where the Galerkin method comes into play.

The Galerkin method, named after the Russian mathematician Boris Galerkin, is a method for converting a weak formulation of a problem into a system of algebraic equations. This is done by choosing the test functions from the same space as the trial (approximation) functions used to represent the solution. When the test functions are chosen differently from the trial functions, the method is referred to as a Petrov-Galerkin method. This approach was named after Boris Petrov, who extended Galerkin's ideas, allowing for more flexibility and stability in the numerical solution, especially in cases where using the same function space for trial and test functions may not be ideal.

The Finite Element Method utilizes these principles by discretizing the domain into smaller, simpler parts called finite elements. Within each element, the solution is approximated by simpler functions, and the weak formulation is applied. The collection of all such elements forms a system of equations that approximate the entire problem.

The Petrov-Galerkin method is particularly valuable in FEM for handling problems where standard Galerkin methods would be insufficient or inefficient. It allows for tailored choices of test functions that can stabilize the solution process or improve the convergence properties of the method.

Understanding the weak formulation and its implementation through the Petrov-Galerkin approach is crucial for appreciating not just the mathematical elegance but also the practical utility of the Finite Element Method in tackling a wide range of scientific and engineering challenges.
